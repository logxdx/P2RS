Abstract

This report comprehensively details the design process of a reliable bus schedule informa- tion system. We aim to empower commuters with informed decisions through accurate real-time updates on bus arrival time to a stop, including anomalies. We focus on fast and cost-effective reproducibility when additions need to be made to the system. Power efficiency and low maintenance requirements are critical considerations in ensuring dura- bility. We have provided cost estimates to scale this to a network of any size.

  1. Project Statement

A campus has a bus service to carry residents within campus. The number of buses can be from 0 to k (presently there are TWO buses, so k = 2). There are two endpoints (East Campus Market and Department of Management Studies (DMS) TIFAC Building). The buses do not display their labels and users wait in the appropriate bus stop on the correct side of the street and take the next bus travelling in the direction they want to go. When there is more than one bus available, each bus starts with staggered starting time. Travel times between stops is approximate and may vary by at most 1 minute. The k buses could be said to be running in a circular route because they turn around at the end points and run back over the same roads. Their bus stops are roughly (not exactly) on opposite sides of the road and denoted by a steel pole with the picture of a bus or a cement pole with a embossed inverted triangle. At the end points, a bus waits for 5 minutes before starting back again (it never starts earlier than a designated starting time).

  1. Motivation

We found that people on the IIT Delhi campus prefer other modes over using the bus because of the perceived unreliability of the bus service, including unpredictable arrival times. Hence, people prefer ways where the time of reaching their destination is under their control, which includes electric autos, cycling, or simply walking. The bus network has a running cost, regardless of people boarding it. By making this schedule well- informed, it can save costs for commuters, which is the intention of the bus system. This will also reduce carbon emissions on the campus and be extremely helpful for people with mobility challenges. A working system like this can accommodate anomalies more effectively than the existing system.

  1. Requirements

  1. Stop Unit Requirements

  1. Display Unit

The system should utilize LCD Dsplays with backlighting. Messages should be alternated in English and Hindi to inform users of the next bus arrival time. The capacity and occupancy of the bus needn’t be synced with the display interface.

  1. Visibility

Font size should be optimized for clear visibility up to 8 feet away by individuals with standard 6/6 vision, using flashing displays for displaying the current time within an accuracy of ±1 minute.

  1. Accessibility

Auditory or tactile feedback mechanisms, like Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) outputs or speech Integrated Circuits (IC)s, should be implemented to support visually impaired passengers.

  1. Passenger Detection

A sensor should immediately report any person standing for 30 seconds as a waiting passenger to the bus driver(s). False positives can also be reported.

  1. Mounting and Protection

Displays should be secured at heights resistant to theft and reselling, utilizing me- chanical fixation and IP67 enclosures to withstand typical New Delhi weather con- ditions, especially in summers without thermal breakdown.

  1. Hours of Operation

Display units should operate from 0700 to 1930 hours. Outside these hours, the units should automatically power down and restart the next day.

  1. Power Supply

The display units should be battery-powered, with solar power as an optional backup.

  1. Bus Mounted Unit Requirements

  1. Unit Composition

Each bus should be equipped with a unit featuring a bi-color Light Emitting Diode (LED) (red/green) for service status indication to the driver.

  1. Power Supply and Availability

  • The bus units should be powered by the bus’ battery (primary power) and a separate internal battery (secondary power). The transition between primary and secondary power sources is automatic.
  • Primary power is accessible whenever the bus’ ignition is on or the engine has started, with secondary power covering all other times to maintain uninter- rupted functionality.
  1. Service Indicators

  • Red LED Indicates the bus is out of service. All stops along the route will display a warning to waiting passengers.
  • Red Blinking LED Reserved for future design uses, such as indicating spe- cific error conditions.
  • Green LED Signifies the bus is in normal service mode.
  • Green Blinking LED Alerts that passengers are waiting at one or more bus stops along the route.
  • White LED A white LED is provided for additional meaningful design pur- poses, as determined by project needs.

  1. System Operation Protocols

  1. Bus Operation Protocol
  • Multiple buses can run simultaneously to accommodate passengers above the capacity of a single bus.
  • The bus will be labelled as out-of-service in case of refuelling, washing and other maintenance tasks. Maintenance tasks will be carried out on a daily basis.
  • The bus shouldn’t stop on empty bus stops and should operate on its specified route.
  • A bus needn’t be notified about the location of other buses.

  • The presence of a waiting passenger for more than 30 seconds should trigger a sensor, alerting the bus unit that passengers are waiting via a blinking bi- colour LED.
  1. Exception Handling Protocol
  • A specific code (e.g. ‘999’) should be used to indicate emergencies or unex- pected delays, informing passengers effectively.
  • Any malfunction in the stop unit or BUk should be rectified within 15 minutes of detection.

  1. Data Collection Protocol

  • The bus departure and arrival time at each stop should be logged automatically.
  • Onset and duration of out-of-service time should be logged.
  • Location and travel activity details should be logged automatically.
  • Data logged should be stored for a minimum of 1 week to a maximum of 1 month.

  1. Miscellaneous Requirements

  1. Connectivity
  • The system should prefer connectivity options that ensure continuous opera- tion, with limited use of Global System for Mobile Communications (GSM) and WiFi only when necessary for updates or address acquisition.
  • Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) is permitted for communication between units.
  • Wireless data transmission security protocols can be integrated.
  1. Scalability
  • Installing a stop unit for a new bus stop and powering it should be enough to add a new bus stop to the route.
  • Pre-information, such as the new stop unit ID, should be pre-fed into the system to minimize setup time.
  • The maximum time required to add a new bus or stop should be 1 hour.

  1. Ignition Control for the BUk Unit

Figure 4: BUk Unit timing diagram

The key requirements and specifications for an Micro Controller Unit (MCU)-based module for ignition control are:

  1. MCU: ESP32 microcontroller
  2. Low Power Consumption: <2mW when just monitoring ignition signal
  3. Detection Capability: Able to detect 12V DC and 24V DC ignition signals
  4. Control Features: Capable of controlling power enabling/disabling sequences with delays
  5. Boot/Shutdown Signals: Can generate PWRBTN# pulses for system boot/shut- down

Power Input:

  • Primary power from bus’s ignition/engine (when running) - 12V DC
  • Secondary power from internal battery (when ignition is off)

Power Output:

  • 3.3V and 5V standby power rails for other components of BUk system
  • Utilizes simple DC-DC buck converters to generate these from primary/secondary inputs

The LM2596 Adjustable DC-DC Step-Down Buck Converter IC TO263-5 is a suitable choice. This IC is available at a price of 27.

Key Specifications

  1. Input Voltage: 4.5V - 40V DC, covering the range of the primary power supply (12V DC or 24V DC from the bus’s ignition/engine)

Figure 5: LM2596 Adjustable Module

  1. Rated Current: 0.2 - 3A, capable of driving up to 3A load, sufficient for the system’s needs
  2. Efficiency: 73%
  3. Number of Pins: 5, standard for this type of IC
  4. Output Voltage Range: 1.23V - 37V
  5. Switching Frequency: 150kHz, beneficial for smaller-sized filter components and efficiency
  6. Features: Thermal shutdown and current limiting protection
  7. Package Options: Available in a standard 5-lead TO-220 package with several different lead bend options, and a 5-lead TO-263 surface mount package

  1. Power Specification

  • MCU power: ≤ 2mW when monitoring
  • Buck converter efficiency losses during conversion
  • Actual power drawn depends on current requirements of the 3.3V and 5V rails

  1. Space Requirements

  • Compact design, including MCU, buck converters, and associated passives
  • Estimated to fit within a package of 5cm × 5cm or smaller

  1. Adjustable Output

  • The LM2596’s adjustable output feature allows for precise voltage regulation.
  • This ensures that the 5V standby power rail meets the exact requirements of the system components.

  1. Communication between Bus and Bus Stop

One of the most important things is establishing communication between the bus and the bus stop. For this, we need—communication from the bus, communication with the bus stop, and data relay between the bus and the bus stop.

  1. Communication from the Bus

  • Each bus has sensors that track its speed.
  • The communication module in the bus is used to transfer data from the bus to the bus stop.

  1. Communication to the Bus Stop

  • Each Bus stop has sensors that detect approaching buses and receive the data transmitted from the bus.
  • The sensors would obtain information about the distance of the approaching bus, its current status of arrival.

  1. Data relay between the Bus and the Bus Stop

  • When a bus approaches a stop, the relevant data, such as time, proximity, and speed, are communicated to the bus stop via sensors installed in the bus.
  • Similarly, the bus stop could communicate information about the number of pas- sengers waiting and any other special requests, along with performing a ‘reset’ for the sensor to prepare it for the next stop.

  1. Possibility of a central communication hub?

  1. Power Devices

  1. ESP32 Board: The ESP32 is a powerful microcontroller module commonly used in IoT applications. Here are the power specifications:
  • Operating Voltage: The ESP32 operates typically at 3.3 volts (V). However, it can tolerate a voltage range from 2.2V to 3.6V. It is important not to exceed this range to avoid damaging the board.
  • Power Consumption: The power consumption of the ESP32 varies depend- ing on its operating mode and the tasks it’s performing. In sleep mode, it can consume as little as a few microamps, while during active WiFi transmission, it can consume up to several hundred milliamps.
  • Power Supply: The ESP32 Dev Board can be powered via USB or an exter- nal power source connected to the VIN pin. When powered via USB, ensure that the source can provide sufficient current, typically around 500mA. For external power, the voltage should be regulated within the specified range.
  1. Battery Charger Module: Battery charger modules usually have a specified input voltage range within which they can operate. This range might vary, but common ranges include 5V for USB-powered modules and wider ranges for modules intended for other power sources.
  2. Voltage Regulator IC: Voltage regulator ICs are crucial components in electronic circuits for stabilizing and regulating voltage levels. Here are some typical power specifications and considerations for voltage regulator ICs:
  • Input Voltage Range: Voltage regulator ICs have a specified input voltage range within which they can effectively regulate the output voltage. For linear regulators, this range is usually limited, while switching regulators may have a wider input voltage range.
  • Output Voltage: Voltage regulator ICs provide a stable output voltage re- gardless of variations in the input voltage or load conditions. The output voltage is typically specified in the datasheet and can be fixed or adjustable depending on the specific IC.
  1. Female DC Power Jack: Female DC power jacks are commonly used connectors for providing power to electronic devices. Here are the typical power specifications and considerations for female DC power jacks:
  • Voltage Rating: Female DC power jacks are rated for a specific voltage range. Common voltage ratings include 5V, 9V, 12V, and 24V, although other voltages are also available depending on the application. It’s crucial to choose a power jack with a voltage rating that matches the requirements of the device being powered.
  • Current Rating: The current rating of a female DC power jack specifies the maximum amount of current it can safely handle without overheating or caus- ing damage. Current ratings typically range from a few hundred milliamps to several amps, depending on the size and design of the connector. It’s essential to select a power jack with a current rating that exceeds the maximum current consumption of the device.

  1. Step-Up Boost Converter Module: Step-up boost converter modules are used to increase the voltage level from a lower input voltage to a higher output voltage. Here are the typical power specifications and considerations for these modules:
  • Input Voltage Range: Step-up boost converter modules have a specified input voltage range within which they can effectively operate. This range typically starts from a minimum input voltage and extends up to a maximum input voltage. Common input voltage ranges might be, for example, 2V to 24V or 3V to 35V.
  • Output Voltage: The output voltage of a step-up boost converter module is the voltage level it boosts the input voltage to. Output voltage can usually be adjusted within a certain range, though some modules have fixed output voltages. Common output voltage ranges might be, for example, 5V to 35V or 12V to 45V.
  1. LED: LEDs (Light Emitting Diodes) are widely used for various lighting applica- tions due to their energy efficiency, long lifespan, and compact size. Here are the typical power specifications and considerations for LEDs:
  • Forward Voltage (VF): Forward voltage is the voltage required to turn on the LED and allow current to flow through it. This voltage varies depending on the type of LED and the color emitted. Common forward voltage ratings for LEDs range from approximately 1.8 volts to 4 volts.
  • Involvement Factor (IF): Forward current is the current flowing through the LED when it is operating. Exceeding the maximum forward current rating can lead to overheating and damage to the LED. Typical forward current ratings for LEDs range from a few milliamps to several hundred milliamps, depending on the LED’s size and type.

  1. Network Design

The communication architecture involves every stop communicating with every other stop and every bus in the network. It uses a ESP32 module on each bus and each stop. The communication between modules is via RF signals. Nearby bus from stop is detected by the RSSI strength of the signals. It will take the signal from the ESP device on the bus to capture the bus arrival and then send it to the next bus stop. This ensures the system’s scalability and ensures a peer-to-peer communication network is established. This helps in system robustness. The following graph shows the communication network for 2 buses and 3 stops:

Figure 9: Network Chart for 2 Buses and 3 Stops

  1. Control Flow

  • Stop Detection: As the bus comes within a range of 10 meters (RSSI < 60) of the stop, and bus stop connection is made, and data is transferred.
  • Bus Stop Display: As soon as the bus stops, which is detected by MPU, the timer on that stop goes to 0. Now, as the bus starts leaving, the timer is updated on all the stops.
  • Detection of bus stopping between runs: This is detected by MPU and WiFi strength. If the bus is stationary (by MPU), and has a weak/no RSSI strength with the stop, it is considered to be stopped in between.

Figure 10: Control Flow of our design. Communication that are wired are depicted by a line without arrows. Otherwise, arrows depict the direction of signal sent.

  • Estimation of Bus Delay and updating the bus timer: If the bus is in range of the next stop, the timer of all stops is paused. If the bus is not in range, it stores the time for which it was stationary. This offset is applied to timers as soon as the bus connects to a stop.
  • Emulating Out of Service: This is controlled by pushButton of BUk. As the LED is set to OutOfService mode, the bus sends a signal to the stop. This signal is sent to all the stops and timers show OutOfService simultaneously.
  • Nearest Bus Identification: We have determined a way to identify exactly when a bus has left a stop. So, we know the last stop that the bus has left and the time it left. We can then predict the time to reach any other stop, as the stop order is fixed.
  • Data Logging: We use the EEPROM library to log data. We store the waiting time at each bus stop, in the format [BUS STOP ID, WAITING TIME].
